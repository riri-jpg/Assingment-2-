The Cartographer of Ordinary Places
She mapped the places no one else would bother:
the alley where the light turns amber-green,
the bench where pigeons gather after four,
the exact point where two tired rivers lean.
She drew the laundromats in careful ink.
She measured every underpass and drain.
She noted where the sound of traffic softened,
and where it came back louder after rain.
Her maps were full of things that no one named —
the mood of certain corners after dusk,
the way the park smells different near the fountain,
the texture of the air near evening, musk
and petrol, bread and something underneath it,
something old and loamy, almost sweet.
She said the world is made of unmapped places
hiding in plain sight beneath your feet.
She gave them names. She drew their careful borders.
She made a legend: yellow meant alive.
Blue meant quiet. Red meant nearly sacred.
Green meant: this is where things still survive.
When she died, they found a thousand pages —
a whole city folded into squares.
Nobody knew what half the symbols meant.
But everybody recognized their squares.Sonnet 4.6
