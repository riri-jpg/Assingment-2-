The Man Who Collected Sunsets

Gerald Fitch had forty-three notebooks, each one filled with sunsets.
Not photographs. Not paintings. Just words. He'd been doing it since 1987, when he was thirty-one and his wife had left him on a Thursday and he'd stood on the fire escape of his apartment in a city that didn't care about him and watched the sky turn a colour he had no name for. Something between grief and tangerine. He'd gone inside and written it down because he felt that if he didn't, it would disappear entirely, and the world would be poorer for it, and nobody would ever know.
He kept going after that.
He moved to a smaller apartment, then a larger one, then a house in a suburb that smelled of cut grass and mild disappointment. He had jobs — something in insurance, briefly something in a hardware store, a long stretch in an office where the windows faced north and he had to walk two blocks on his lunch break just to see the sky properly. He did this without fail for eleven years.
People thought he was strange. His neighbour Douglas thought he was very strange indeed and said so, often, near the bins. Gerald nodded and agreed and went back inside and wrote about the way the clouds had looked like torn newspaper over the motorway that evening, pink-edged and purposeful.
He never showed the notebooks to anyone.
